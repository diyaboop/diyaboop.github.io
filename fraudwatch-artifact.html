Artifact 01

Written Outline — What It Does & Why It Matters

The Problem

Standard fraud detection models report aggregate performance metrics — AUC, precision, recall — without surfacing who gets wrongly flagged. A model with 92% recall can simultaneously impose a 2.7× higher false positive rate on debit card users than credit card users. That disparity is invisible in any standard dashboard. It is also a CFPB UDAAP exposure.

What I Built

  • Training module: Random Forest classifier trained on 590,540 real IEEE-CIS transactions. Feature engineering across 394 anonymized variables. Outputs fraud_model.pkl + features.pkl.
  • Bias audit module: Post-hoc fairness analysis using Z-tests and p-values across card type, product category, and billing region. Stores structured audit results in SQLite.
  • Drift monitor: Weekly cohort comparison against a baseline window. Flags population shift, score distribution drift, and false positive rate changes before they compound.
  • API layer: FastAPI serving five endpoints — /metrics, /audit, /monitor, /predict, /health — consumed by the dashboard frontend and externally testable.
  • Live dashboard: Single-page frontend pulling live data from the API. Deployed via Docker on Render. No manual refresh required.

Key Finding (the one the model hid)

Credit card users faced a 56.8% false positive rate vs 34.3% for debit card users — a statistically significant disparity (Z = 66.6, p < 0.001). Product category showed an even starker gap: 82.3% vs 30.8% FPR (Z = 109.1, p < 0.001). Neither finding appeared in standard model evaluation. Both have direct regulatory implications under ECOA and CFPB UDAAP.

Tangible Impact

  • Identified 45,375 false positives that a production deployment would have acted on — wrongly blocking legitimate transactions.
  • Surfaced 2 bias findings at p <0.001 that were completely absent from standard performance reporting.
  • Zero drift alerts missed across 12 monitored cohort windows — giving continuous assurance rather than point-in-time auditing.
  • Mapped all findings to 4 regulatory frameworks (ECOA, EU AI Act Art.10–13, CFPB UDAAP, SR 11-7) so compliance teams have a ready-made remediation brief.

Why This Matters for AI-Native Workflows

FraudWatch demonstrates the exact pattern that high-stakes AI deployments need but rarely have: a persistent, automated accountability layer that runs alongside the model — not as a one-time audit but as a live system that catches problems before they become incidents. The same architecture applies to any revenue-impacting AI system: lead scoring, churn prediction, pricing algorithms, or account prioritization.
